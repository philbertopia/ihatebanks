## Why options instead of shares

Buying 100 shares of a stock like Apple at $145 costs $14,500. Buying a call option that controls those same 100 shares costs a fraction of that — a few hundred to a few thousand dollars depending on the strike and expiration.

This is the core leverage advantage of options. You can participate in the upside of a stock move without committing the full capital required to own the shares. If the stock moves up $10, the option holder captures most of that gain on a much smaller investment.

The tradeoff: if the stock does not move — or moves the wrong way — you can lose the entire premium paid. There is no "riding it out" the way a shareholder can. **Options have an expiration date.** That clock is always running.

---

## Reading momentum as an entry signal

The simplest entry signal for directional options is momentum: what direction is the stock already moving?

**Positive momentum** — the stock is trending up, making higher highs, and price gaps above the previous close. This is the setup for a long call.

**Negative momentum** — the stock is trending down, making lower lows, or gapping down on volume. This is the setup for a long put.

A stock gapping up from $103.81 to $106.96 on the open is a textbook positive momentum signal. The gap tells you institutions are buying aggressively. Riding that momentum with a call option captures the continuation of the move.

The key word is **already moving**. You are not predicting where a stock will go — you are identifying where it is already going and getting positioned in that direction.

## Strike selection: why in-the-money matters

Options are available at many different strike prices. Most beginners buy cheap out-of-the-money options because they cost less. This is a trap.

**Out-of-the-money (OTM) options** have a delta below 0.50. A delta of 0.10 means the option has roughly a 10% chance of finishing in the money. Cheap price, low probability.

**In-the-money (ITM) options** have a delta above 0.50. A delta of 0.54 means the option has a greater-than-50% chance of finishing in the money at expiration. More expensive, higher probability.

For swing trades targeting 1-5 day moves, ITM options are better for two reasons:

1. **Higher delta** — the option moves more per dollar of stock movement. A 0.54 delta call gains $0.54 for every $1 the stock rises. A 0.10 delta call gains only $0.10.
2. **Higher probability** — your directional thesis needs to be correct, but the odds start in your favor rather than against you.

A 0.54 delta call on Apple with a $145 strike and $8.70 premium means:
- Maximum loss: $870 per contract
- Break-even at expiration: $145 + $8.70 = $153.70
- The stock needs to move to $153.70 for you to break even at expiry — but you do not hold to expiry. A 30-40% gain on the option premium in 2-3 days is the target for a swing trade, not waiting for expiration.

## Expiration date selection

For swing trades (1-5 days), choose an expiration at least **7-21 days out**. This gives the trade room to work without expiration-day gamma crushing the position.

Same-day (0DTE) options are far cheaper but extremely risky for directional swing trades — they have maximum gamma, meaning a small adverse move can wipe out the entire premium within minutes.

**General rule:**
- Earnings play: buy the expiration that captures the earnings date, typically 1-5 days out
- Non-earnings swing trade: buy at least 7-14 days out to reduce time decay pressure

## Earnings setups

Earnings reports are one of the highest-probability setups for buying directional options. When a company reports earnings, the stock often makes a large single-day move — 5-15% or more — in one direction.

If you can correctly anticipate the direction before earnings, buying an ITM call (bullish) or ITM put (bearish) the day before can produce a double on your premium overnight.

**How to assess direction:**
- Is the stock trending up into earnings, suggesting positive expectations?
- Has the company consistently beaten estimates in recent quarters?
- Is the sector showing strength or weakness?
- Is there negative momentum and analyst downgrades heading into the report?

None of these signals are perfect. Earnings is inherently uncertain — even companies that beat estimates can fall on "sell the news" reactions. For this reason, **earnings options positions should be sized smaller than normal swing trades**, because the binary outcome means you are right or wrong with no opportunity to manage during the event.

## Buying puts for downside moves

Everything above applies to put options in reverse. Puts profit when the stock falls.

If you identify a stock with negative momentum — breaking below support, gapping down, reporting weak earnings — buying an ITM put with delta near -0.50 gives you a position that profits as the stock falls.

The mechanics are identical to calls:
- Pay a premium upfront
- Maximum loss is the premium paid
- Profit if the stock moves in your direction before expiration
- Break even at strike minus premium paid (for puts)

Puts are often slightly more expensive than equivalent calls due to "skew" — the market prices in more downside risk — but the concept is the same.

## Risk management: the only rule that matters

Options can lose 100% of their value. This is not a rare edge case — it happens regularly to traders who ignore position sizing.

The rule is simple: **only trade what you are willing to lose entirely.**

If your account is $2,000, do not risk $2,000 on a single option trade. A reasonable starting allocation is 10-25% of your account per trade — the amount you could lose completely without affecting your ability to continue trading.

This creates a framework:
- $2,000 account → risk $200-$500 per trade
- Take the loss if it hits your stop (typically 20-30% of premium paid)
- Move to the next trade with the remaining capital

The traders who succeed long-term are not the ones who win the most on individual trades. They are the ones who are still trading after 100 trades — still in the game, still learning, still improving.

Entry: stock closes >1.5% above prior close. Buy slightly ITM call (delta ~0.55), DTE 21-45 days.
Exit: 80% profit target, 35% stop loss, or 5-day maximum hold.

| Metric | Result |

| Avg loss | -$309 |

| Total return | -2.1% |
| Max drawdown | 8.8% |
| Sharpe ratio | -1.19 |

**What the results show:** The strategy fired on real gap-up signals but the 80% profit target was rarely hit within the 5-day window. The kill switch activated after 29 trades due to negative expectancy (-0.46R per trade), which blocked further entries. The 212% spread cost ratio (wide synthetic bid/ask) consumed most gains on winners.

**Key limitation:** This backtest uses synthetic end-of-day prices as a gap proxy. Real gap-up trades enter on the morning open using the prior close as reference. End-of-day data captures a different — and noisier — signal than true opening gaps.

### What this means for the video strategy

The video strategy is designed for real-time execution with:

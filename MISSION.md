# Mission

## 💔 I Hate Banks
### Open Research in Algorithmic Options Strategies

I Hate Banks is an open research initiative dedicated to the transparent study, development, and evaluation of systematic options trading strategies.

This project was founded on the belief that much of modern quantitative finance, despite being built on publicly available mathematics, statistics, and computing, remains unnecessarily opaque and institutionally gated.

For decades, advanced research in derivatives trading and algorithmic strategy design has largely been confined to hedge funds, proprietary trading firms, and large financial institutions. While the tools of quantitative finance have become increasingly accessible through modern computing and open data, practical knowledge around implementation often remains hidden behind institutional barriers.

This project exists to challenge that model.

I built this project as an artist who felt marginalized and bullied by the banking and finance industry. I became a quant to learn how to stop depending on institutions that gatekeep financial knowledge and concentrate power.

I Hate Banks documents systematic trading strategies with a focus on:

- transparency
- reproducibility
- empirical evaluation

Each strategy published on this platform is accompanied by clear methodology, documented assumptions, and reproducible backtests. Performance is evaluated using standardized quantitative metrics, including Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, profit factor, and out-of-sample validation, to reduce selective reporting and improve research quality.

Rather than presenting strategies as opaque black boxes, the goal is to treat them as research artifacts: systems that can be studied, tested, critiqued, and improved through open collaboration.

The strategies explored here focus primarily on options-based systematic trading, including credit spreads, stock replacement structures, volatility-aware approaches, and long-duration LEAPS exposures.

All materials in this project, including research notes, strategy logic, backtesting frameworks, and documentation, are released under open-source licenses. Anyone is encouraged to study the work, replicate experiments, fork the repository, and contribute improvements.

The intention is not to present definitive answers about markets, but to create a public laboratory for quantitative strategy research.

At its core, I Hate Banks is about expanding access to financial knowledge.

This project is for educational purposes only and is not financial, legal, or tax advice.

Markets reward discipline, evidence, and experimentation.  
Those principles should be open to everyone.
